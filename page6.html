Suburbia hell to me was Cypress.
I knew this place was hell the moment I was placed in a class that was meant to help me improve my English language skills (ESL) in the 2nd grade.

I never really fit in that town. There was minimal diversity, which meant zero ecuadorians.
There wasn’t people who had my hair or my eyes.
Kids can be so cruel and my earliest memories here where of being stripped from my native language and being bullied at school for having “puffy hair and big eyes”.
As i got older, the more out of place I felt. At home and school.
There is a heavy cultural difference and disconnect between parents and first gen kids. I wasn’t exactly an easy kid either.
I was curious, rebellious, I was too out-spoken and argumentative. In a town where everyone is a carbon copy of each other, it felt like resistance to preserve myself.
Can you imagine what it was like for Ecuadorian parents to have a daughter like this?
I didn’t understand why I was so different or didn’t fit in. I couldn’t even understand why I was so angry at the world. Some call it a phase or teenage angst, but I just knew I couldn’t exist as me here.

And despite always feeling out of place in that town, this was home too. It is where my parents still live and its a place that gave me courage.

Courage to be and courage to leave.

It’s also where i developed my greatest gift — daydreaming.

I spent hours daydreaming about moving back to New York but to make that reality happen, I knew I had to figure out a way to leave Houston.

And thats how I ended up in Austin.
